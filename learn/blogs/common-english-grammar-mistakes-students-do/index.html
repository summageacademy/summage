Common English Grammar Mistakes Learners Make

Most Common Grammar Errors and How to Fix Them

Many students understand English quite well but still make small grammar mistakes while speaking or writing. These small mistakes can affect your IELTS score negatively in writing and speaking parts.

In this blog, we will show you some of the most common grammar mistakes students make and explain how to fix them in a simple ways.

1. Article Mistakes (a / an / the)

Wrong: I saw a dogs in the park

Correct: I saw dogs in the park / I saw a dog in the park

Explanation: Articles (a, an, the) are used with only singular and countable nouns. That's why "a dogs" is incorrect. Well, there are certainly difference between these two.

Also, many students think "an" is used only before vowel letters like (a, e, i, o, u). But this is not actually correct as it also depends on the sound. For example: "an hour" (because the "h" is silent) and "a university" (because it sounds like "yu" sound at the start).

2. Subject–Verb Agreement

Wrong: She go to school every day

Correct: She goes to school every day

Explanation: In the present simple tense, verbs change depending on the subject and I think most of us know that pretty well. But appearantly, even if we are aware of this grammar rule, some of us still repeat this very mistake. So next time before you use present simple, remember that when the subject is "he", "she" or "it", you usually add "-s" or "-es" to the verb.

For example: "He works", "She studies", "It runs". If you forget this rule, your sentence sounds incomplete or unnatural.

Be careful with longer sentences too! "My brother, who lives in London, works in a bank." The main subject is still "my brother" so the verb must match it. Remember to detect the main subject in the sentence so it is easier to implement the grammar rule.

3. Tense Mistakes

Wrong: Yesterday I go to the market

Correct: Yesterday I went to the market

Explanation: Many of us must realize that any type of Tenses actually just show time. If you are talking about the past, you must use past tense verbs. There is also a very simple trick to determine any tense. For example, Words like "yesterday", "last year", "ago" or "in 2020" clearly tell you the action is in the past.

A common problem is usually mixing different tenses in one sentence. For example: "Yesterday, I went to the store and buy some food"... But it should be "bought" because both actions happened yesterday, which is in the past.

Try to always check when did this happen so then it is easier to choose the correct tense.

4. Using “Many” and “Much” Incorrectly

Wrong: I don’t have much friends

Correct: I don’t have many friends

Explanation: The difference between "many" and "much" depends on whether the noun is countable or uncountable.

Use "many" with things you can count: friends, books, cars.

Use "much" with things you cannot count: water, money, time, sugar and you name it.

If you can count the item, just simply use "many". If you clearly can not, then use "much".

6. Preposition Mistakes

Wrong: I am good in English

Correct: I am good at English

Explanation: Prepositions (in, on, at, to, for, etc.) are small but difficult to actually use because they don't always follow any clear rules.

For example: "good at", "interested in", "afraid of", "depend on". You cannot always translate them directly from your native language.

The best way to learn how to use them is simply by memorizing essential phrases used with prepositions. The other ones follow naturally as your mind gets used to them.

7. Double Negatives

Wrong: I don't know nothing

Correct: I don't know anything

Explanation: In standard English, we usually use only one negative word in a sentence as using two negatives might confuse the meaning.

Naturally it is better to use "I don’t have anything" or "She didn’t go anywhere" rather than using "something" or "somewhere" instead.

They are not correct for exams like IELTS even though double negatives exist in some spoken forms.

Final Tips

In simple words, You need to practice and notice your mistakes. You do not need to sit down and memorize all these grammar rules perfectly but

Here are some simple tips:

• Write short paragraphs every day

• Read and pay attention to sentence structure and try to use learned structures more often

• Listen to how native speakers form sentences and try to "shadow" their structure

• Review your mistakes and try not to repeat them next time

Most importantly, don't be afraid to make mistakes. As we mentioned in our other blogs, how can you expect to improve if you do not make any mistakes at all. You must see mistakes as lessons and actually try to learn from them..
